Heuristics for Achieving Personal Development

April 2, 2025 · Growth

How I Think About Personal Development

Why it matters: Personal development has been a huge unlock for me—I can now do things I couldn't before. But it's systematically neglected because it's not urgent and easy to deprioritize.

My biggest unlock: Improving my well-being—mental health work massively expanded what I could achieve. After seeing the results, I became more bullish on other forms of personal development.

Some examples of stuff I've worked on:

  • Creating a vision for a team
  • Learning Squiggle
  • Doing the AI Safety Fundamentals program
  • Practicing coaching and managing managers
  • Improving communication

I haven't been perfect, but paying attention and putting in steady effort really helped.

Heuristics That Help Me Stick With It

1. Tie it to your current work

You're most likely to succeed if there's a clear link to your actual projects and ultimate goals.

Example: I wanted to improve communication. I was already making meeting docs, so I used those as practice for clarity.

It's harder to prioritize if it's a totally separate, one-off project.

2. Build it into your routines and accountability

Reducing friction is key—there are too many blockers otherwise. My weekly agenda includes things like:

  • "Did you meditate every day?"
  • "What's something you worked on in coaching this week?"

Eventually it becomes part of your rhythm.

Doing things with others helps too! Reading groups, accountability partners—but only when people are actually holding each other accountable.

3. Find opportunities within existing goals

Identify something you're already doing this quarter. Ask: Can I put extra attention into it to also grow in a personal development area?

Example: I had a talk coming up, so I put more time into it to work on communicating themes clearly.

Learning from Others

Books were hit-or-miss until I got better framing: no book is perfect—skim lots, take what makes sense, build your own model.

My system now:

  • Audiobooks while walking, doing dishes, etc.
  • Jot down notes and "lessons learned"
  • Buy physical copies later for highlighting and sticky notes

This helps me absorb and refer back to stuff more easily.

Final Thoughts

I've seen people (myself included) go from "area of weakness" in one performance review to "area of strength" in the next. That only happens with:

  • Clear goals
  • Integration into your actual work
  • Consistent follow-through
These improvements compound over time. If you get 10% faster and do it for two years? That adds up.
